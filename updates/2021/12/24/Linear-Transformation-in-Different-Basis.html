Linear Transformation in Different Basis

Posted by Faisal Ghaffar on December 24, 2021 · 6 mins read

A vector $u$ is characterised with respect to a basis of a coordinate system. The basis provide a reference that is used to associate numbers with geometric vectors. In the $\mathbb{R}^2$ Cartesian plane, a vector $u=\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$ is considered as a linear combination of the standard basis vectors $i=\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0\end{bmatrix}$ and $j=\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ as:

\[u = 3i + 2j\]

Now, to represent this vector $u$ in a different coordinate system, basis of the vector needs to be changed. Imagine we rotate the basis by $\theta$ degree which can be envisaged as new coordiante system, then new basis are determined as:

\[i' = i.\cos \theta + j.\sin \theta \\ j' = i.(-\sin \theta) + j.\cos \theta\]

We will write this in matrix form as:

\[\begin{bmatrix} i' \\\\ j' \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos \theta & \sin \theta \\ -\sin \theta & \cos \theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} i \\\\ j \end{bmatrix}\]

To have new basis as columns, we transpose matrices.

\[\begin{bmatrix} i' \\\\ j' \end{bmatrix}^\intercal = \begin{bmatrix} i \\\\ j \end{bmatrix}^\intercal\begin{bmatrix} \cos \theta & \sin \theta \\ -\sin \theta & \cos \theta \end{bmatrix}^\intercal\] \[\begin{bmatrix} i' & j' \end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix} i & j \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos \theta & -\sin \theta \\ \sin \theta & \cos \theta \end{bmatrix}\]

This new matrix on the right hand side is called change of basis matrix and each column of this matrix is a basis vector of the new basis. Let’s denote it with $X$:

\[X = \begin{bmatrix} \cos \theta & -\sin \theta \\ \sin \theta & \cos \theta \end{bmatrix}\]

A change of basis matrix $X$ (which contains the basis vectors as columns) maps an input basis $B_I = \begin{bmatrix} i & j \end{bmatrix}$ to an output basis $B_O = \begin{bmatrix} i’ & j’ \end{bmatrix}$ as:

\[B_O = B_I.X\]

From this, we can calcualte the change of basis matrix given input and output basis by multiplying both sides with inverse of $B_I$ as given below:

\[B_O = B_I.X \\ B_I^{-1}B_O = B_I^{-1}B_I.X \\ B_I^{-1}B_O = I.X \\ X = B_I^{-1}B_O\]

How to change the basis of a vector?

Let’s express a vector $u$ as linear combination of the input and output basis vectors as:

\[\begin{cases}u & =c_1.i + c_2.j \\ u & =d_1.i' + d_2.j'\end{cases}\]

The scalars $c_1$ and $c_2$ are weighting the linear combinations of the input basis vectors, and the scalars $d_1$ and $d_2$ are the weights for linear combination of output vector. By merging the two equations for $u$ and writing in matrix form, we get:

\(\begin{bmatrix} i & j \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c_1 \\\\ c_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} i' & j' \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} d_1 \\\\ d_2 \end{bmatrix}\) where $\begin{bmatrix} i & j \end{bmatrix} = B_I$ is the input basis and $\begin{bmatrix} i’ & j’ \end{bmatrix}=B_O$ is the output basis.

Let’s denote vector $u$ in output basis as $[u]{B_O}$ and in input basis as $[u]{B_I}$

\[\implies B_I.[u]_{B_I} = B_O.[u]_{B_O}\]

To calculate the vector components in the output basis $B_O$, by multiply both sides by $B_O^{-1}$ we get:

\[B_O^{-1}B_I.[u]_{B_I} = B_O^{-1}B_O.[u]_{B_O}\] \[[u]_{B_O} = B_O^{-1}B_I.[u]_{B_I}\]

$B_O^{-1}B_I$ is the inverse of $B_I^{-1}B_O$ which is the change of basis matrix $X$ described before. In other words, inverse of change of basis matrix ($X^{-1}$) allows us to convert vectors from an input basis $B_I$ to an output basis $B_O$ and $X$ from $B_O$ to $B_I$.

If $B_I$ is standard basis, means \(X = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\), then:

\[[u]_{B_O} = B_O^{-1}.I.[u]_{B_I}\] \[[u]_{B_O} = B_O^{-1}.[u]_{B_I}\]

How to transorm a vector in new basis?

Consider a matrix $A$ associated to a tranformation $T$, when applied to a vector $u$ in basis $B_1$, produces a transformed vector in basis $B_1$ denoted as $[v]_{B_1}$. \([v]_{B_1} = A[u]_{B_1} \tag{1}\)

When vector $u$ is represented in another basis, say $B_2$, a change of basis matrix $X$ is applied as discussed in previous section.

So vector $u$ in basis $B_2$ denoted as $[u]_{B_2}$ is given as:

\([u]_{B_2} = X[u]_{B_1}\) To transform $[u]_{B_2}$ with changed basis to another vector $v$, we need to apply a transformation matrix (say $D$) relative to basis $B_2$. The tranformation will be:

\[[v]_{B_2} = D[u]_{B_2}\]

The transormation matrix $D$ is unknown and needs to be determined in new basis $B_2$.

There is an alternative way to determine the transfomed vector $[v]_{B2}$. Steps are listed below:

  • Convert the vector back to basis $B1$ to produce $[u]{B_1}$. This is done by applying change of basis matrix $X$ to $[v]{B_2}$ as:
\[[u]_{B_1} = X[v]_{B_2} \tag{2}\]
  • Apply tranformation matrix $A$ to transform $[u]{B_1}$ to $[v]{B_1}$ as per equation 1.
\[[v]_{B_1} = A[u]_{B_1} \tag{3}\]
  • Replace $[u]_{B_1}$ in equation 3 with right hand side part of equation 2,
\[[v]_{B_1} = AX[v]_{B_2} \tag{4}\]

  • Change the basis of $[v]_{B_1}$ from $B_1$ to $B_2$ by applying inverse of change of basis matrix $X^{-1}$ \([v]_{B_2} = X^{-1}[v]_{B_1}\)

    Replace $[v]_{B_1}$ with right hand side of equation 4. We get:

    \[[v]_{B_2} = X^{-1}AX[v]_{B_2}\]

However, we already have transformed vector $v$ in basis $B_2$ as:

\([v]_{B_2} = D[u]_{B_2}\) \(\implies D= X^{-1}AX\)

With this, we have determined the transormation matrix $D$ in new basis corresponding to a transformation (represented by $A$) in old basis through just the change of basis matrix $X$ and its inverse $X^{-1}$.

This $D= X^{-1}AX$ can be interpreted as decomposition of a matrix $D$ into matrices $X$ and $A$. It has many applications in machine and deep learning whihc we will discuss in future posts.
